The Current UK Economy

For the year 2023-24 there was a big black hole in spending. the UK Income tax and non tax income was £1.06 trillion. For the same year the UK Government spend was £1.23 trillion. The deficit was £132.2 billion.

The UK current debt is £2.72 trillion which is now 101.3% of UK GDP. The annual interest paid is £104.9 billion which is 8.2% of the total government income. An optimistic forecast made by the government on future borrowing is:

  1. 2025–26: £137.3 billion
  2. Corporations
  3. Trade unions
  4. Lobbyists with specific interests

This relies on government stable interest rates, spending less and UK economic growth. Alongside this challenge is our ageing population and low birth rates.

  1. By 2030 20% of the UK population will be over 65 putting increasing pressure on the public finances through pensions, health and care costs.
  2. In 2025-21% are under18; 62% are working and aged between 18-64; and 17% are 65 and over.
  3. In 2030-20% are under18; 60% are working and aged between 18-64; and 20% are 65 and over.

The result of this demographic change is that there is forecast to be a further gap between income and expense of £20-30 billion annually by 2030.

How are the government tacking these challenges?

In the spring 2025 statement the following initiatives were stated. The UK government’s main economic initiatives include:

  • Infrastructure: Investing £13 billion in infrastructure and training 60,000 skilled workers.
  • Housing: Allocating £2 billion for social housing and enabling 170,000 new homes.
  • Defence: Increasing defence spending to 2.5% of GDP by 2027.
  • Tax Reforms: Tackling tax avoidance and adjusting welfare spending.
  • Green Economy: Aiming for 95% clean power by 2030 and supporting green technologies.
  • Regional Growth: Fostering local investments and strategic partnerships.
  • Economic Stability: Maintaining fiscal sustainability and supporting growth.
Are they enough?

Maybe?

We could have some unforeseen bit of good fortune which seems to have been the usual reason for the UK economy to growing quickly in the past. Historically successful government initiatives have often been “short term gain for long term pain”

The UK's past economic growth has largely been driven by deregulation and public borrowing, both of which have provided short-term benefits but created long-term challenges:

  • Deregulation: Increased investment and business activity, especially in finance, but led to greater financial instability (e.g., 2008 crisis) and rising costs in privatised sectors.
  • Public Borrowing: Stimulated economic growth and prevented recessions, but resulted in high national debt, limiting future spending options.

The key debate is whether the UK should continue relying on these strategies or shift toward structural reforms that promote sustainable growth.

Do we now need to be more proactive and ambitious and own/control our future? I think we may now need plans that are more tangible and measurable. We maybe need to be innovative and try something new to relaunch UK business.

Time to “think outside of the box” and try some innovative initiatives that may make the difference..?

1. The UK National Innovation Fund

Summary

The UK National Innovation Fund is a public private investment vehicle that deploys £5.0 billion of investable capital to back UK startups and scaling companies. The government provides £2.0 billion and private investors provide £3.0 billion. The fund follows private equity practice: management fees and a contingency reserve are paid from investment returns, and qualifying private investor distributions are tax free under strict rules. The design aims to maximise capital in the market, attract experienced managers, create skilled jobs across the country, and deliver a net benefit to the public purse when the fund performs.

How the fund works

TThe fund pools £5.0 billion and outsources investment decisions to proven venture capital and private equity managers selected through competition. When portfolio companies generate returns, the fund first pays management fees and sets aside a contingency reserve for losses. The remaining distributable returns are split pro rata between the government and private investors. Private investors receive tax free distributions only if they meet eligibility rules such as minimum holding periods and substance tests. The government’s receipts include its share of distributable returns plus additional tax revenue from new jobs, with clawbacks applied if agreed conditions are not met.

Where the money goes

Sectors targeted include green energy, technology and AI, life sciences, advanced manufacturing, and creative industries. Regions prioritised include the North of England, the Midlands, Scotland and the South West. The fund aims to provide patient capital for companies that can scale, create high skill jobs and attract follow on private investment.

Financial modelling plain summary

A simple five year model shows likely outcomes under three return scenarios. Key assumptions are transparent and changeable: £5.0 billion investable pool, management fees 1.5 percent per year, contingency 5 percent of gross returns, government share 40 percent, private share 60 percent, private returns tax free for qualifying investors, and illustrative job tax receipts.

Scenario Gross returns five years Fees five years Contingency five Government net five years
Conservative £1.25bn £375m £62.5m +£1.23bn
Moderate £2.50bn £375m £125m +£3.56bn
Optimistic £3.75bn £375m £187.5m +£4.89bn

The model treats fees and contingency as paid from gains rather than from extra public cash. Government net equals the government’s share of distributable returns plus job tax receipts minus the notional tax foregone on private returns and minus the £2.0 billion government investment. Even under the conservative scenario the model shows a positive net for the public purse because of job tax receipts and the government’s share of returns. Higher returns increase the public benefit substantially.

Plain takeaways and safeguards
  • The fund puts £5 billion to work in UK companies while managers are paid from the gains they generate.
